The History Of Cereal

It's sordid and full of flavor.

  • In 1863, James Caleb Jackson, a religiously conservative vegetarian who ran a medical sanitarium in western New York, created a breakfast cereal from graham flour dough that was dried and broken into shapes so hard they needed to be soaked in milk overnight. He called it granula.

  • 1920s - A health clinician accidentally spilled a wheat bran mixture onto a hot stove, creating what would come to be called Wheaties. Its famous slogan, “Breakfast of Champions,” would first appear on a billboard for a minor league baseball team in Minnesota in the 1930s.

  • 1950s - After World War II, cereal consumption increased with the advent of the baby boom, and sugar became a selling point. Kellogg’s invented Frosted Flakes and its pitchman, Tony the Tiger, and a new era of television advertising began.

  • 1970s - The heyday of fruit-flavored and monster cereals filled children’s bowls with Count Chocula, Franken Berry and Boo Berry. At the same time, the Federal Trade Commission began taking a harder look at how cereal companies marketed their products to children.

  • 1990s-2000s - The battle of the virtuous cereals was on. Kellogg’s acquired the Kashi line, just one sign of the exploding market for natural and organic foods. Cereals consumers demanded more label transparency and products without genetically modified/artificial ingredients.

  • Current Day - The number and diversity of cereals has never been so vast. Grab a spoon, a bowl and some cold milk - It's time to enjoy.
